## Subject 1: Internet of Things (IoT)

**Q1. What is the definition of IoT?**

The Internet of Things (IoT) refers to the network of physical devices, vehicles, appliances, and other objects embedded with sensors, software, and connectivity that enables them to collect and exchange data over the internet without direct human intervention.

IoT bridges the physical and digital worlds, allowing everyday objects to become "smart" and communicate autonomously. A simple example is a smart thermostat that adjusts temperature based on your habits, or a wearable that monitors your heart rate and sends data to your doctor in real time.

**Key elements of an IoT system:**
- **Sensors/Devices** — gather raw data from the environment
- **Connectivity** — transmit data via Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, 4G/5G, etc.
- **Data Processing** — cloud or edge systems analyze the data
- **User Interface** — dashboards or apps display results and allow control

IoT is driving innovation across industries — smart cities, precision agriculture, connected healthcare, industrial automation, and intelligent transportation are all powered by IoT. Over 15 billion devices are currently connected globally, and this number is growing rapidly.

Challenges include security vulnerabilities, privacy concerns, device interoperability, and managing the enormous volume of data generated by billions of connected devices simultaneously.

---

**Q2. List some key characteristics of IoT.**

IoT systems are defined by a unique set of characteristics that set them apart from traditional internet-connected systems.

**Core characteristics:**

- **Interconnectivity** — Any object can be connected to the global information network, forming a massive web of communicating devices.
- **Heterogeneity** — IoT devices are built on different hardware platforms, operating systems, and networks, yet must seamlessly interact with each other.
- **Dynamic and Self-Adapting Behavior** — Devices continuously adjust their operations based on context. For example, a security camera may switch to high resolution when motion is detected.
- **Self-Configuring Capability** — Devices can join networks, update firmware, and configure themselves with minimal human setup.
- **Unique Identity** — Each device has a unique identifier (e.g., an IPv6 address) to distinguish it globally.
- **Enormous Scale** — The sheer number of devices and the volume of data generated far exceeds traditional internet systems.
- **Sensing** — Devices collect physical parameters like temperature, humidity, pressure, and motion, converting them into digital signals.
- **Energy Efficiency** — Many IoT devices run on batteries in remote locations, requiring optimized power consumption.
- **Safety** — Since IoT controls physical systems, security is critical — a compromised device can have real-world consequences.

---

**Q3. Describe the IoT Conceptual Framework.**

The IoT Conceptual Framework provides a structured understanding of how data flows from physical devices to actionable insights. It outlines five key stages:

1. **Gather** — Physical sensors embedded in devices collect raw environmental data such as temperature, GPS coordinates, motion, or humidity levels. This is the entry point of the IoT data pipeline.

2. **Enrich** — Raw data is enriched with contextual metadata — timestamps, device IDs, location information — that increases its analytical value and traceability.

3. **Stream** — Enriched data is securely transmitted over a communication network (Wi-Fi, 4G/5G, Zigbee, LoRa) to cloud servers or edge computing nodes for further processing.

4. **Manage** — Cloud platforms store and analyze the data using algorithms and machine learning models to identify patterns, anomalies, and trends. This is where intelligence is applied.

5. **Acquire** — Actionable insights are extracted and delivered to users or automated systems as alerts, dashboards, commands, or reports for decision-making.

This framework promotes **modularity** — each stage can be improved independently. It also supports **edge computing**, where processing occurs near the device rather than sending all data to the cloud, which reduces latency and bandwidth usage significantly in time-critical applications.

---

**Q4. Explain the IoT Architectural View.**

The IoT architectural view describes the layered structure that enables data to flow from physical devices to end-user applications. It is typically organized into four layers:

**1. Perception Layer (Device Layer)**
The lowest layer consisting of physical things — sensors, RFID tags, actuators, cameras, and smart meters. Sensors capture real-world data while actuators execute physical commands (e.g., opening a valve, turning on a light).

**2. Network Layer**
Responsible for transmitting data from devices to processing units. It uses communication technologies such as Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, Zigbee, 4G/5G, and Ethernet. This layer ensures reliable, secure data transport.

**3. Processing Layer (Middleware/Edge Layer)**
Data is temporarily processed, filtered, and aggregated here. Edge and fog computing nodes at this layer reduce cloud load and enable faster local decisions — critical for real-time applications like autonomous vehicles.

**4. Application Layer**
The topmost layer where processed data becomes useful services — smart home apps, industrial dashboards, healthcare monitoring platforms, and agriculture management systems.

Some architectures add a **Business Layer** for managing policies, service monetization, and privacy compliance. Understanding this architecture helps designers make informed decisions about scalability, security, and latency optimization across the entire IoT ecosystem.

**Q5. What constitutes the Physical Design of IoT?**

The physical design of IoT defines the actual hardware components and low-level communication protocols that form the foundation of any IoT system.

**Core hardware components:**

- **Microcontroller/Microprocessor** — The brain of the device (e.g., Arduino, ESP32, Raspberry Pi) that runs firmware and processes sensor data.
- **Sensors** — Detect physical parameters: temperature, humidity, motion, light, pressure, GPS.
- **Actuators** — Execute physical actions based on commands (motors, relays, valves).
- **Communication Modules** — Wi-Fi chips, Bluetooth transceivers, Zigbee radios, or cellular modules for wireless data transfer.
- **Power Supply** — Batteries, solar panels, or wired power; energy efficiency is critical for remote deployments.
- **Memory/Storage** — Flash memory and SD cards for buffering data locally.

**Networking protocols by OSI layer:**
- **Link Layer** — IEEE 802.11 (Wi-Fi), IEEE 802.15.4 (Zigbee), Ethernet
- **Network Layer** — IPv4/IPv6 for device addressing
- **Transport Layer** — TCP (reliable) or UDP (low-latency streaming)

The physical form factor — PCB size, enclosure material, and ruggedness — also matters, especially for industrial or outdoor deployments where devices face harsh environmental conditions.

## Subject 2: Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning

**Q1. What is Artificial Intelligence?**

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is a branch of computer science focused on building systems that can perform tasks requiring human-like intelligence — reasoning, learning, problem-solving, understanding language, and recognizing patterns.

AI was formally established as a discipline at the 1956 Dartmouth Conference by John McCarthy and Marvin Minsky. It is broadly classified into:

- **Narrow AI (Weak AI)** — Designed for specific tasks. Examples: facial recognition, spam filters, chess engines, recommendation algorithms.
- **General AI (Strong AI)** — A theoretical concept where a machine can perform any intellectual task a human can. Not yet achieved.

**Key AI techniques:**
- **Machine Learning** — Systems learn from data without explicit programming
- **Deep Learning** — Neural networks with multiple layers, enabling image recognition and language understanding
- **Natural Language Processing** — Understanding and generating human language
- **Computer Vision** — Interpreting visual information from the world

**Real-world applications:** Virtual assistants (Siri, Alexa), autonomous vehicles, medical diagnosis systems, fraud detection, and strategic game playing (AlphaGo defeating world champions).

AI also raises important ethical concerns — bias in decision-making, job displacement, lack of transparency, and risks of autonomous systems making critical decisions without human oversight.

**Q2. What are Production Systems in AI?**

A Production System is a rule-based computational model used in AI to simulate intelligent problem-solving. It is one of the earliest and most influential knowledge representation frameworks, closely mimicking the way humans apply rules to reason and make decisions.

**Three core components:**

**1. Production Rules**
A set of IF-THEN rules encoding domain knowledge:
> IF (condition is true) THEN (perform action)
> Example: "IF temperature > 100°C THEN trigger cooling system"

**2. Working Memory (Global Database)**
A dynamic data store holding the current state of the world — known facts, observations, and intermediate results. This is the system's "awareness" at any point in time.

**3. Control Strategy (Inference Engine)**
The mechanism that drives the system forward through a **recognize-act cycle**:
- **Recognize** — Scan all rules, find those whose conditions match working memory
- **Select** — Use conflict resolution (priority, recency, specificity) to pick one rule
- **Act** — Execute the rule's action, updating working memory; repeat until goal is reached

**Examples:** Expert systems like MYCIN (medical diagnosis) and DENDRAL (chemical analysis) were built on production systems.

**Advantages:** Modularity — rules can be added or removed independently; Transparency — the fired rule sequence explains how conclusions were reached.

---

**Q3. State the characteristics of Production Systems.**

Production systems possess several defining characteristics that make them effective for AI problem-solving and knowledge representation.

**1. Modularity**
Each production rule is self-contained. Rules can be added, removed, or modified independently without rewriting the entire system, making maintenance straightforward.

**2. Uniform Representation**
All knowledge is encoded in a consistent IF-THEN format, ensuring predictability and ease of understanding across the entire knowledge base.

**3. Separation of Knowledge and Control**
Domain knowledge (the rules) is kept separate from the inference mechanism (the control strategy). This allows each to evolve independently, improving flexibility.

**4. Transparency and Explainability**
The sequence of rules fired to reach a conclusion can be traced and audited. This makes production systems valuable in high-stakes domains like medicine and law, where decision explanations are required.

**5. Natural Modeling of Human Reasoning**
IF-THEN rules closely mirror the intuitive way humans reason, making it easier for domain experts to encode their knowledge.

**Limitations:**
- Performance degrades as the rule base grows — matching all rules against working memory in every cycle is computationally expensive (combinatorial explosion).
- The **Rete Algorithm** was developed specifically to address this matching inefficiency.
- **Non-determinism** arises when multiple rules fire simultaneously, requiring conflict resolution strategies (priority, recency, specificity).

**Q4. Explain Breadth-First Search (BFS).**

Breadth-First Search (BFS) is a graph and tree traversal algorithm that explores nodes **level by level**, starting from the root and visiting all nodes at the current depth before moving to the next level.

**How it works:**
- Uses a **FIFO (First-In-First-Out) queue**
- Enqueue the start node → dequeue a node → check if it's the goal → enqueue all unvisited neighbors → repeat
- Continues until the goal is found or all nodes are explored

**Properties:**

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Completeness | ✅ Always finds a solution if one exists |
| Optimality | ✅ Finds the shortest path (fewest edges) |
| Time Complexity | O(b^d) — b = branching factor, d = solution depth |
| Space Complexity | O(b^d) — stores all nodes at current level |

**Strengths:** Guarantees the shortest path in unweighted graphs; systematic and exhaustive.

**Weaknesses:** Memory-intensive — can store exponentially many nodes for deep solutions.

**Applications:** Shortest route finding, solving puzzles (8-puzzle, Rubik's cube), peer-to-peer networking, web crawling, social network analysis (finding shortest connection between people).

BFS is ideal when solutions are expected to be close to the root. For very deep or infinite search spaces, its memory requirements become a major bottleneck.

---

**Q5. Explain Depth-First Search (DFS).**

Depth-First Search (DFS) is a traversal algorithm that explores a graph or tree by going **as deep as possible** along each branch before backtracking to explore alternative paths.

**How it works:**
- Uses a **LIFO (Last-In-First-Out) stack** — either explicit or via the call stack in recursive implementations
- Visit a node → mark it visited → recursively explore each unvisited neighbor → backtrack when a dead end is reached

**Properties:**

| Property | Value |
|---|---|
| Completeness | ✅ In finite spaces (with visited tracking) |
| Optimality | ❌ May find a longer path before the shortest |
| Time Complexity | O(b^m) — m = maximum depth |
| Space Complexity | O(b × m) — far more efficient than BFS |

**Strengths:** Memory-efficient — only stores the current path and unexplored branches.

**Weaknesses:** Not optimal; can get stuck in very deep or infinite branches without depth limiting.

**Applications:** Solving mazes, topological sorting, cycle detection, generating permutations, constraint satisfaction problems (Sudoku, N-Queens).

**Variants:**
- **Depth-Limited Search (DLS)** — caps DFS at a set depth to avoid infinite loops
- **Iterative Deepening DFS (IDDFS)** — combines DFS's memory efficiency with BFS's optimality by progressively increasing the depth limit

## Subject 3: Compiler Design

**Q1. What is a Compiler?**

A compiler is a system software program that translates source code written in a high-level programming language (such as C, Java, or Python) into an equivalent low-level target language — typically machine code or assembly — that a computer processor can directly execute.

The key distinction from an **interpreter** is that a compiler translates the **entire program at once before execution**, while an interpreter translates and executes line by line. This gives compiled code a significant **speed advantage** at runtime.

**Phases of compilation:**
1. **Lexical Analysis** — Breaks source code into tokens
2. **Syntax Analysis** — Verifies grammar rules; builds a parse tree
3. **Semantic Analysis** — Checks meaning (type compatibility, scope)
4. **Intermediate Code Generation** — Creates a platform-independent IR
5. **Code Optimization** — Improves IR for speed and memory
6. **Code Generation** — Produces final machine code

**Error reporting** is a critical function — the compiler provides detailed diagnostic messages for syntax, semantic, and logical errors to help developers debug.

**Examples of compilers:** GCC (C/C++), Javac (Java), Clang (C/C++), MSVC (Microsoft C++).

A well-designed compiler not only translates code correctly but also optimizes it, resulting in programs that run faster and use less memory than handwritten assembly in many cases.

---

**Q2. Briefly explain the major data structures in a compiler.**

Compilers rely on several specialized data structures throughout the compilation pipeline to efficiently analyze, transform, and generate code.

**1. Token Stream**
Output of lexical analysis — a sequence of tokens (keywords, identifiers, literals, operators) extracted from the source code character stream.

**2. Parse Tree / Concrete Syntax Tree**
Built during syntax analysis; represents the grammatical structure of the source program according to the language's formal grammar rules.

**3. Abstract Syntax Tree (AST)**
A simplified, cleaner version of the parse tree that omits redundant grammar details. It is the primary internal representation used in semantic analysis and code generation.

**4. Symbol Table**
The most critical data structure — stores information about all identifiers (variables, functions, classes): their names, data types, scopes, memory locations, and attributes. Accessed and updated throughout all compiler phases.

**5. Intermediate Representation (IR)**
Platform-neutral code forms like **Three-Address Code (TAC)** or **Static Single Assignment (SSA)** used during optimization and code generation.

**6. Control Flow Graph (CFG)**
Represents program flow using **basic blocks** (straight-line code sequences) as nodes and directed edges for jumps and branches. Critical for optimization analysis.

**7. DAG (Directed Acyclic Graph)**
Used within basic blocks to identify and eliminate redundant computations during local optimization.

These structures collectively enable systematic, efficient, and correct transformation of source code into optimized executable programs.

---

**Q3. What are the types of Compilers?**

Compilers are classified based on their structure, purpose, and the relationship between source and target languages.

**1. Single-Pass Compiler**
Reads source code and generates target code in one sequential pass. Fast but limited in optimization capability.

**2. Multi-Pass Compiler**
Makes multiple passes over the source program, with each pass performing a specific task (lexical analysis, optimization, code generation). Enables thorough optimization.

**3. Cross-Compiler**
Runs on one machine architecture (host) but generates code for a different target architecture. Essential for embedded and mobile development — e.g., compiling on a PC to produce code for an ARM microcontroller.

**4. Source-to-Source Compiler (Transpiler)**
Translates source code from one high-level language to another. Examples: TypeScript → JavaScript, CoffeeScript → JavaScript.

**5. Just-In-Time (JIT) Compiler**
Compiles code at runtime immediately before execution, combining portability with near-native speed. Used in Java's JVM and .NET's CLR.

**6. Bootstrapping Compiler**
A compiler written in the same language it compiles. Used to rebuild and verify the compiler itself.

**7. Incremental Compiler**
Recompiles only the changed portions of a program, reducing build times during iterative development.

**8. Optimizing Compiler**
Focuses on generating highly efficient target code through constant folding, loop unrolling, and dead code elimination.

---

**Q4. Differentiate between the Front-end and Back-end of a Compiler.**

A compiler is divided into two major logical halves — the **front-end** and the **back-end** — each handling different aspects of the compilation process.

**Front-End (Machine-Independent / Analysis Phase)**

The front-end reads and analyzes the source language:

- **Lexical Analysis** — Scans source code and produces a token stream
- **Syntax Analysis** — Parses tokens to verify grammar; builds a parse tree / AST
- **Semantic Analysis** — Checks type correctness, scope rules, and declaration consistency
- **Intermediate Code Generation** — Produces a platform-neutral IR

The front-end is **language-dependent but machine-independent**. The same front-end can target multiple architectures.

**Back-End (Machine-Dependent / Synthesis Phase)**

The back-end takes the IR and produces target code:

- **Code Optimization** — Improves IR through dead code elimination, loop transformations, and register allocation
- **Code Generation** — Maps IR to actual machine instructions
- **Target-Specific Optimization** — Applies low-level refinements like peephole optimization

The back-end is **machine-dependent but language-independent**. Multiple language front-ends can share one back-end.

**Why this separation matters:**
This modular design promotes **portability and reusability**. For example, GCC supports multiple source languages (C, C++, Fortran) and multiple target architectures through this architecture.

---

**Q5. What is the Analysis-Synthesis model of compilation?**

The Analysis-Synthesis Model is a foundational conceptual framework that divides compilation into two complementary phases, describing how source code is systematically transformed into target machine code.

**Analysis Phase (Front-End)**

Breaks down and understands the source program:

- **Lexical Analysis** — Scans source characters, groups them into tokens (keywords, identifiers, operators), discards whitespace and comments
- **Syntax Analysis** — Verifies the token stream obeys the language grammar; constructs a hierarchical parse tree
- **Semantic Analysis** — Validates meaning: type compatibility, scope rules, identifier declarations; annotates the AST with type information

Throughout this phase, the **Symbol Table** is built and maintained, and **error messages** are generated for any violations found. The output is an **Intermediate Representation (IR)** of the program.

**Synthesis Phase (Back-End)**

Builds the target program from the IR:

- **IR Optimization** — Transforms the IR to improve efficiency without changing program behavior
- **Code Generation** — Maps IR to machine instructions; handles register allocation and memory layout
- **Machine-Dependent Optimization** — Applies final target-specific refinements

**Why this model is important:**
- The analysis phase can be **reused across different target architectures**
- The synthesis phase can be **reused for different source languages** sharing the same IR
- It makes compiler design **modular, maintainable, and portable**

## Subject 4: Natural Language Processing

**Q1. What is Natural Language Understanding (NLU)?**

Natural Language Understanding (NLU) is a subfield of NLP and AI focused on enabling machines to comprehend the **meaning, intent, and context** of human language — not just process its surface form.

While NLP broadly covers tasks like tokenization and text generation, NLU specifically targets the deeper **semantic and pragmatic understanding** of language.

**Core NLU tasks:**
- **Intent Recognition** — Determining the purpose behind an utterance (e.g., booking a ticket vs. asking for weather)
- **Entity Extraction** — Identifying specific information: names, dates, locations, organizations
- **Coreference Resolution** — Understanding that pronouns refer to earlier-mentioned entities
- **Semantic Role Labeling** — Determining who did what to whom
- **Sentiment Analysis** — Identifying emotional tone (positive/negative/neutral)
- **Relation Extraction** — Identifying relationships between entities

NLU powers conversational AI systems like **Siri, Alexa, Google Assistant**, and enterprise chatbots.

**Challenges in NLU:**
- **Ambiguity** — words with multiple meanings ("bank" — river bank vs. financial bank)
- **Sarcasm and irony** — literal meaning vs. intended meaning
- **Implicit context** — what's unsaid but understood

Modern NLU uses transformer-based models (**BERT, GPT**) that leverage pre-training on massive corpora to develop rich, contextual language representations, significantly improving accuracy over traditional rule-based approaches.

---

**Q2. What are the key components of an NLP pipeline?**

An NLP pipeline is a sequential chain of processing steps that transforms raw text into structured, meaningful information that machines can analyze and act upon.

**Pipeline stages:**

**1. Text Acquisition & Preprocessing**
Collect raw text and clean it — remove HTML tags, special characters, and handle encoding issues.

**2. Tokenization**
Split text into individual units (tokens) — usually words or sentences.

**3. Stop Word Removal**
Filter out high-frequency, low-information words like "the," "is," "and."

**4. Lowercasing & Normalization**
Standardize text to a consistent format.

**5. Stemming / Lemmatization**
Reduce words to their root or base forms (e.g., "running" → "run").

**6. Part-of-Speech (POS) Tagging**
Assign grammatical tags (noun, verb, adjective) to each token for syntactic context.

**7. Named Entity Recognition (NER)**
Identify and classify proper nouns: people, organizations, locations, dates.

**8. Parsing**
Analyze grammatical sentence structure — dependency or constituency trees.

**9. Word Embeddings**
Convert tokens into dense numerical vectors (Word2Vec, GloVe, BERT) capturing semantic relationships.

**10. Downstream Task**
Execute the specific NLP task: sentiment analysis, machine translation, question answering, or text summarization.

Each step is critical — errors in early stages propagate downstream and can significantly degrade final model performance.

**Q3. Differentiate between Stemming and Lemmatization.**

Stemming and Lemmatization are both text normalization techniques that reduce words to a common base form, but they differ significantly in approach and output quality.

**Stemming**
A crude, rule-based method that chops off word endings using simple suffix-stripping rules, without understanding grammar or meaning.

- "running" → "run" ✅
- "studies" → "studi" ❌ (linguistically incorrect)
- "happiness" → "happi" ❌

**Algorithms:** Porter Stemmer, Snowball Stemmer

**Pros:** Very fast and computationally cheap
**Cons:** Often produces non-dictionary, meaningless base forms

---

**Lemmatization**
A linguistically informed process that reduces a word to its canonical dictionary form (lemma) by considering the word's part of speech and meaning.

- "better" → "good" ✅
- "was" → "be" ✅
- "running" → "run" ✅

**Tools:** WordNet, spaCy, NLTK Lemmatizer

**Pros:** Semantically accurate; always produces real, meaningful words
**Cons:** Slower and computationally more intensive

---

**When to use which:**

| Scenario | Preferred Method |
|---|---|
| Large-scale search / IR | Stemming |
| Chatbots, QA systems | Lemmatization |
| Sentiment analysis | Lemmatization |
| Speed is critical | Stemming |

**Q4. What is a Bag-of-Words model?**

The Bag-of-Words (BoW) model is a foundational text representation technique in NLP that converts documents into fixed-length numerical vectors based on word occurrences, completely ignoring grammar, word order, and sentence structure.

The term "bag" reflects that the document is treated as an unordered collection of words — structure is discarded.

**How it works:**
1. Build a **vocabulary** of all unique words in the corpus
2. Represent each document as a vector of the same length as the vocabulary
3. Each position's value = the word's **frequency** in that document

**Example:**
- Doc 1: "The cat sat on the mat"
- Doc 2: "The dog sat on the mat"
- Vocabulary: [cat, dog, mat, on, sat, the]
- Doc 1 vector: [1, 0, 1, 1, 1, 2]
- Doc 2 vector: [0, 1, 1, 1, 1, 2]

**Variant — TF-IDF:** Improves BoW by weighting words higher if frequent in a document but rare across the corpus, reducing dominance of common words.

**Advantages:** Simple, easy to implement, effective for spam detection and basic classification.

**Limitations:**
- Loses semantic relationships (king ≠ queen in BoW)
- High-dimensional sparse vectors
- No context or word meaning

Word embeddings (Word2Vec, BERT) were developed specifically to overcome these fundamental limitations.

**Q5. Explain the concept of Sentiment Analysis.**

Sentiment Analysis (also called Opinion Mining) is an NLP technique that identifies and classifies the **emotional tone or opinion** expressed in a piece of text — typically as **positive, negative, or neutral**.

**Levels of analysis:**
- **Document-level** — Overall sentiment of an entire document (e.g., a movie review)
- **Sentence-level** — Sentiment of individual sentences
- **Aspect-level** — Sentiment about specific features: *"The camera is great but the battery life is terrible"* (positive for camera, negative for battery)

**Approaches:**

**1. Lexicon-Based**
Uses pre-built sentiment dictionaries (VADER, SentiWordNet) where words carry pre-assigned sentiment scores. Fast and interpretable but rigid.

**2. Machine Learning**
Trains classifiers (Naive Bayes, SVM, Logistic Regression) on labeled datasets. Better accuracy than lexicon methods.

**3. Deep Learning**
Uses LSTM networks, CNNs, and transformer models (BERT, RoBERTa) to capture contextual sentiment with state-of-the-art accuracy.

**Applications:**
- Brand reputation monitoring on social media
- Customer review analysis
- Public opinion tracking on news events
- Employee satisfaction surveys

**Challenges:**
- Sarcasm: *"Oh great, stuck in traffic again!"*
- Negations: *"Not bad"* ≠ negative
- Context dependency and multilingual text

**Q1. Find the next number in the series: 2, 6, 12, 20, 30, ?**

**Answer: 42**

**Pattern 1 — Differences between terms:**
- 6 − 2 = 4
- 12 − 6 = 6
- 20 − 12 = 8
- 30 − 20 = 10
- Next difference = **12** → 30 + 12 = **42**

**Pattern 2 — Formula n(n+1):**
- 1×2 = 2, 2×3 = 6, 3×4 = 12, 4×5 = 20, 5×6 = 30, **6×7 = 42**

Both methods confirm the answer is **42**.

**Q2. If 'APPLE' is coded as 'ELPPA', how is 'ORANGE' coded?**

**Answer: EGNARO**

**Pattern:** The word is simply **reversed**.
- APPLE → reversed → E-L-P-P-A = **ELPPA** ✅

Applying the same rule to ORANGE:
- O-R-A-N-G-E → reversed → **E-G-N-A-R-O**

**Answer: EGNARO**

**Q3. A man is facing North. He turns 90° clockwise, then 180° counter-clockwise. Which direction is he facing?**

**Answer: West**

**Step-by-step:**
1. Start: **North**
2. Turn 90° clockwise: North → **East**
3. Turn 180° counter-clockwise: East → North → **West**

**Final direction: West**

**Q4. If 5 workers can build a wall in 10 days, how many days will 2 workers take?**

**Answer: 25 days**

**Using Man-Days:**
- Total work = 5 workers × 10 days = **50 man-days**
- With 2 workers: 50 ÷ 2 = **25 days**

This is an **inverse proportion** — fewer workers means more days.

**Answer: 25 days**

**Reasoning:**
